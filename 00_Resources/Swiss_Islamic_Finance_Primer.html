Section 1Finance perspective

The core thing Saad is building is called a Crypto AMC. Here's what that means, piece by piece.

AMC — Actively Managed Certificate A financial product issued as a certificate (think of it like a voucher backed by real assets). You buy one certificate and you're effectively buying exposure to a basket of assets that a manager actively picks and adjusts. It's similar to a mutual fund or ETF, but structured differently under Swiss law. The key word is "actively managed" — someone is making ongoing decisions about what goes in and out of the basket.
ISIN — International Securities Identification Number Every regulated financial product in the world gets a unique 12-digit code. If a product has an ISIN, it's real, registered, and can be bought and sold on exchanges. Think of it like a passport number for a financial product. Saad's product will have one.
SIX — Swiss Exchange Switzerland's national stock exchange. One of the oldest and most respected in the world. If his AMC is listed there, it has gone through regulatory review and has institutional credibility behind it. This is not a crypto exchange — it's a proper securities exchange like the London Stock Exchange or NYSE, but Swiss.

So when Saad says "Swiss Crypto AMC with ISIN listing on SIX" — he means: a regulated, Swiss-issued financial product with a unique ID, tradeable on a real stock exchange, that holds a basket of altcoins managed by someone making active decisions.

Why altcoins? Why no Bitcoin?

BTC is excluded. This is likely a Shariah screening decision — Bitcoin is so dominant and speculative that some scholars argue a BTC-only or BTC-heavy product lacks the productive underlying activity needed to be halal. Altcoins tied to specific blockchain utility (DeFi protocols, infrastructure tokens, etc.) can be screened on a case-by-case basis. It's also a product differentiation play — if your users want Bitcoin exposure, you already provide that. A managed altcoin product is genuinely additive.

On-chain version (Q4 2026) The second phase of what he's building. Instead of a certificate sitting on a Swiss exchange, the same product exists as a token on a blockchain. Anyone with a crypto wallet or app integration can hold it. This is what he means by "can be plugged into any crypto app for distribution" — Fasset could technically add it as a tap-to-buy product in the app, similar to how you'd add a new coin.
Finance TL;DR Saad is building a Swiss-regulated, Shariah-certified investment product that holds altcoins. Phase 1 is a traditional financial instrument on a real exchange. Phase 2 is the same thing but native to crypto. Both phases need a distribution partner to reach customers — that's where Fasset comes in.

Section 2Regulatory perspective

This is the part that gets complex. Three different regulatory environments matter here.

1. The Swiss side (his problem, not yours)

Switzerland doesn't have Islamic finance-specific laws, but its general financial law (overseen by FINMA, the Swiss regulator — equivalent to the SEC in the US) is flexible enough to allow Shariah-compliant structures. Structured certificates like AMCs are a well-established product type there. What Saad has done is build his product inside this framework and then layered Shariah certification on top via Amanah Advisors. This is legitimate and well-trodden in European Islamic finance.

2. The distribution side (your problem)

When a Fasset user buys a financial product through the Fasset app, Fasset is acting as a distributor. This requires Fasset to be licensed in that user's jurisdiction to offer that type of product. Here's where it gets nuanced:

UAE (Dubai) VARA — Broker-Dealer Does VARA allow distributing foreign-structured investment certificates? Likely yes, but needs confirmation from Yazan.
Bahrain CBB — Category 3 Crypto-Asset CBB is conservative. Distributing a Swiss AMC may require additional approval.

Indonesia Bappebti (commodity regulator) Indonesia has a whitelist of approved crypto assets. A Swiss AMC is not a crypto asset per se — unclear if it fits.

3. The Shariah side (shared)

This is where it gets genuinely interesting. Saad's Shariah board is Mufti Faraz Adam of Amanah Advisors — the same scholar who has been advising on Fasset's card product. This is a big deal because:

  • He already trusts Fasset — you're not starting from zero
  • If Fasset distributes this product, the Shariah certification is already embedded in the product — you don't need your own scholar to review each holding
  • It creates a joined-up Shariah narrative across Fasset's product suite
Regulatory TL;DR His regulatory homework (Swiss product registration) is largely done. Your question is whether your existing licenses cover distributing this type of product to your users. UAE (VARA) is the most promising starting point. You need your compliance/legal team (Yazan) to confirm before committing to anything.

Section 3Licensing perspective

Think of this as "who is allowed to do what."

The Manufacturer / Distributor model
In traditional finance, there are two distinct roles. The manufacturer creates the financial product (structures it, gets it regulated, manages the assets). The distributor sells it to end customers. Saad is explicitly positioning Swiss Islamic Finance as the manufacturer. He wants Fasset to be the distributor.

This is a common and clean business model. The manufacturer earns fees on AUM (assets under management). The distributor earns a fee — usually called a trailer fee or distribution fee — for every customer it brings in and every year those customers stay invested.

What Fasset would need to do as a distributor

  • Confirm its existing licenses cover this product type in each market (or get additional approval)
  • Integrate the product into the app — either as a traditional product listing (Phase 1 AMC) or as an on-chain integration (Phase 2)
  • Handle customer onboarding, KYC, and any investor suitability requirements
  • Manage the commercial relationship: fee reporting, reconciliation, AUM tracking

What Fasset would NOT need to do

  • Build or manage the investment product itself
  • Hire portfolio managers or investment analysts
  • Get a Swiss license
  • Run the Shariah screening (already done by Amanah Advisors)
Licensing TL;DR Your role is distributor only — much lighter lift than if you were building this product yourself. The main licensing question is whether your current approvals cover distributing a Swiss-registered certificate. Start with UAE/VARA as the test market.

Section 5Business perspective

The market they're both addressing

There are approximately 1.8 billion Muslims in the world. The majority are in markets Fasset already operates in or is expanding to (Southeast Asia, MENA, South Asia). Estimates of Muslim-owned investable assets that currently lack Shariah-compliant options range from $2 trillion to over $7 trillion. This is the market both Fasset and Swiss Islamic Finance are fundamentally going after — from different angles.

How the money works

In a distribution deal, here's roughly how fees flow:

  • User invests, say, $1,000 into the AMC
  • Swiss Islamic Finance charges an annual management fee — typically 1-2% of AUM for an actively managed product
  • Out of that fee, they pay Fasset a trailer fee — usually 0.2-0.5% annually — for every dollar of AUM Fasset brought in and keeps invested
  • So if Fasset's users put $10M into this product, Fasset earns $20,000-$50,000 per year in passive fee income, just for keeping the product on the shelf

These numbers sound small, but at scale (Fasset has 500K+ users) and with a growing AUM base, it becomes meaningful recurring revenue with near-zero marginal cost once integrated.

What stage is Swiss Islamic Finance at?

Honest assessment: early. No public investors, no disclosed AUM, likely pre-revenue or very early revenue. Saad is a credible founder with a real Shariah board, a Swiss legal structure, and a product in development — but this isn't an established fund with a track record. The risk is that the product doesn't launch on schedule or doesn't gain traction.

Why this is still worth exploring

  • The downside is low — a distribution agreement can be non-exclusive with minimal commitment until Phase 2 integration
  • The Mufti Faraz Adam connection means due diligence on the Shariah side is largely pre-done
  • Getting in early means favorable commercial terms — you have more leverage now than if they have $100M AUM in a year
  • Raafi said "open to whatever you ascertain is possible" — this gives you real latitude to shape the deal structure

The one thing to watch

Saad said allocation is "focused on altcoins, BTC will be out." Understand the specific coins in the portfolio and the active management strategy before committing to distributing it. If the portfolio performs badly, it reflects on Fasset's brand — especially with users who are trusting you with halal investments.

Business TL;DR Low-risk commercial exploration with a credible early-stage founder. Start with a light distribution arrangement (Phase 1 referral, no build), use that time to validate demand and get regulatory clarity, then decide on Phase 2 integration if it shows promise. Raafi's mandate is "figure out what's possible" — you have room to move slowly and deliberately.
