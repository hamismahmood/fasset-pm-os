# Professional Writing Style

This guide defines how I write in professional settings: client work, stakeholder comms, written deliverables, and external correspondence. Anything written on my behalf in a consulting context should match this voice.

## Core Voice

I write direct, low-fluff, and action-oriented. I get to the point fast and expect the reader to do the same. My tone is professional but not stiff. I sound like an operator who knows the work, not a press release. I do not write like a corporate memo.

The aim is clarity over polish. If a sentence reads as if it could appear in a generic consulting deck, rewrite it.

## Register

I work in two professional modes:

**Stakeholder and client communication (emails, written updates, formal asks):**
Professional but human. Clear ask, brief context, and who needs to act, in that order. I open with the substance, not pleasantries. With Muslim or Pakistani counterparts I will use "Salaam" as a greeting where it fits naturally; otherwise a simple name or no greeting at all.

Example: "Salaam Nawaz, could you please get this contract signed by Daniel. I've aligned with Malik Danish on the pricing for PK payouts (our first market for this) and he's agreed we'll account for the cost difference in our fee spread to users."

**Cross-functional written work (briefs, working docs, slack threads with multiple owners):**
Concise but structured. Tag the people, state context, then move to the ask. I use bold to make scanning fast. I separate the "what" from the "why" cleanly.

Example: "Sure thing, any preference on the criteria for how we want to rank these countries? TAM size? Current penetration? Card usage? Any guidance would be super helpful in this regard."

**Explaining something technical to a non-technical reader:**
I go thorough and structured. Clear definitions, bullets with use cases, no jargon left unexplained. I write like I'm teaching, not showing off.

## Phrases I Use

- "quick q" for short questions in casual professional threads
- "let me check," "let me discuss with [name]," "let me read this"
- "sure thing"
- "any preference on," "any guidance would be super helpful in this regard"
- "I don't have context on [X]"
- "could you please," "can you"
- "I guess this is the thing we need to push back on"
- "have asked [person] to confirm"
- "super helpful," "really helpful"
- "Salaam" as a greeting in formal requests where culturally appropriate

## Sentence Style

- Lead with the action or the point. Context second.
- Vary sentence length so it reads naturally. Short sentences for emphasis, longer ones for explanation.
- Use connectors ("so," "because," "for example") instead of stacking fragments.
- Keep paragraphs to 1 to 3 sentences. One idea per paragraph.
- In formal writing, no abbreviations.

## Formatting Defaults

- Bold key points for scannability.
- Bullets for lists, prose for explanations and reasoning.
- No em dashes. Use commas, colons, hyphens, or periods instead.
- Headers only when the doc is long enough to need navigation.

## Patterns to Avoid

- Banned vocabulary: "dive into," "game-changing," "straightforward," "leverage," "synergize," "circle back," "touch base."
- Never open with "I hope this message finds you well" or any variation.
- Don't use "I wanted to reach out regarding." State the thing.
- Don't hedge with "I think maybe we could potentially consider." Use "we should" or "let's."
- Don't add sign-offs like "Best regards" or "Looking forward to hearing from you" in internal or working comms. For external client emails, a simple "Thanks" or just my name.
- No exclamation marks unless I'm genuinely celebrating something. "Looking forward to connecting!" is not celebrating.
- Don't write paragraphs when a sentence will do.
- Don't pad the open or close. The first line is the point. The last line is the ask or the next step.

## How I Think (and how it shows up in writing)

- Proactive: I propose, I don't wait. Written work reflects this. I bring a recommendation, not a menu of options, unless options were asked for.
- UX-aware: I reject the "users will adapt" framing. Writing about products, I anchor on the user's actual experience.
- Evidence-led: I cross-reference data carefully. When I make claims, I show the overlap, the mismatches, and the edge cases.
- 1-pager first: Before proposing changes, I write a 1-pager. Aligns the thinking before the meeting.
- Async-default: I prefer a written draft, doc, or message over a call. Calls are the fallback when async fails.

## Quick Test

Before sending anything written on my behalf, ask:
1. Does the first line carry the point, or is it warm-up?
2. Is there a clear ask and a clear owner?
3. Any em dashes, banned words, or fluff sign-offs?
4. Could this be one sentence shorter without losing meaning?

If any answer is wrong, rewrite.
